## Research

My work asks how rivers and the ecosystems within them store and move sediment and carbon —
across scales from a single beaver pond to entire continental river systems, and across
timescales from a field season to millennia. It falls into four themes.

### 1. Calibrating the geomorphic clock
{: #geomorphic-clock}

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/img/geomorphic-transit-model.png" alt="Conceptual model of sediment storage and transit through a migrating floodplain">
  <figcaption>Sediment entering a corridor (Q<sub>s</sub>) is repeatedly stored and remobilized as the river migrates; total transit time is the sum of many storage events and transit distances.</figcaption>
</figure>

The centerpiece of my dissertation is a remote sensing-based approach to modelling how
sediment is stored and routed through alluvial river corridors. Using multidecadal, global
Landsat records, I measure how the world's largest and most dynamic rivers migrate and
rework their floodplains, and I use those measurements to estimate sediment and carbon
residence times over long timescales. The goal is a "geomorphic clock" — a way to read
transient sediment storage and transit times directly from how a river's planform, lithology,
climate, and human alteration shape its movement. This work is supported by
[RivMapper](https://github.com/huck-rees/RivMapper), my Python toolkit for building water
masks and quantifying migration from satellite imagery.

### 2. Sediment and carbon storage in beaver-mediated stream corridors
{: #beaver}

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/img/beaver-methods.png" alt="Methods for quantifying sediment storage behind beaver dams across timescales">
  <figcaption>Repeat geomorphic surveys, historical air-photo time series, and depth-incremented cores with radiometric dating together constrain sedimentation and aggradation rates across timescales — from year-to-year to floodplain-aggradation — as an incised channel reconnects to its floodplain.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure class="photo">
  <img src="/assets/img/beaver-poster.jpg" alt="Huck Rees presenting beaver research at AGU">
  <figcaption>Presenting beaver sediment- and carbon-storage research at AGU.</figcaption>
</figure>

Beaver dams turn narrow mountain streams into complexes of ponds and wet meadows that trap
sediment and bury carbon. Using sediment cores, geomorphic surveys, and drone-based mapping,
I quantify sedimentation and carbon-accretion rates behind beaver dams and ask how they vary
across environments in the American West — and what that means for beaver-based restoration
as a tool for wetland recovery, flood attenuation, and carbon storage.

### 3. Mapping a changing flood pulse in a UNESCO World Heritage Site
{: #okavango}

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/img/okavango-change.png" alt="Map of change in inundation across the Okavango Delta">
  <figcaption>Change in inundation across the Okavango Delta (post- minus pre-2010–2012 La Niña): teal marks wetting, brown marks drying, exposing migrating channels, shrinking lakes, and shifting flow toward the Selinda Spillway.</figcaption>
</figure>

The Okavango Delta in Botswana — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — is sustained by an annual
flood pulse whose timing and reach shape one of the planet's great wetland ecosystems. I
build multidecadal satellite records of the Delta's inundation to track how that pulse is
changing, and to understand the tectonic, climatic, and ecological controls behind it.
Recent work reconstructs a 41-year monthly inundation record that reveals an abrupt
redistribution of flood waters across the Delta, working toward the ability to anticipate
where and when the water will go.

### 4. Flood adaptation in a changing world
{: #flood-adaptation}

<figure>
  <img src="/assets/img/valmeyer-managed-retreat.jpg" alt="Before-and-after maps of managed retreat at Valmeyer, Illinois">
  <figcaption>Managed retreat at Valmeyer, IL: structures in the FEMA 100-year floodplain (1989) were relocated to higher ground after catastrophic flooding, with later construction (2018) concentrated outside the floodzone.</figcaption>
</figure>

<figure class="photo">
  <img src="/assets/img/odanah-managed-retreat.jpg" alt="Fieldwork on a street in Odanah, Wisconsin">
  <figcaption>Fieldwork and stakeholder interviews in Odanah, Wisconsin — a community weighing retreat from repeated flooding.</figcaption>
</figure>

Flooding is among the costliest and most widespread hazards people face, and the choices
communities make — protect, accommodate, or retreat — carry long consequences. I study the
effectiveness of managed retreat and community relocation as flood-mitigation strategies,
from small Midwestern towns to post-tsunami reconstruction in Japan, connecting physical
flood science to the social and policy dimensions of adaptation.
